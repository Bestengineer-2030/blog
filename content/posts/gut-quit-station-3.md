Every Hyrox athlete knows the feeling.

You're 40 minutes in. Wall balls are done. You're moving toward the sled push and suddenly your stomach has opinions. Cramping. Nausea. An urgency that has nothing to do with race strategy.

You didn't eat the wrong thing. Your training is solid. Your legs are fine.

Your gut quit.

## What Is Actually Happening

During high-intensity exercise, your body makes a ruthless prioritisation decision. It redirects up to 80% of blood flow away from your gut toward your working muscles.

Your intestines become temporarily oxygen-deprived.

Three things happen immediately:

**1. Tight junctions loosen.** The cells lining your gut wall are held together by protein structures called tight junctions. Under ischaemia, these loosen — allowing partially digested food and bacterial endotoxins to seep into your bloodstream. Your immune system responds with systemic inflammation. You feel it as nausea, fog, and that general sense that something is wrong.

**2. Motility becomes unpredictable.** Your gut moves contents faster or in uncoordinated waves. This produces urgency and cramping at exactly the moment you cannot stop.

**3. The SkiErg and burpee stations make it worse.** These two stations generate significant intra-abdominal pressure from repeated bending and exertion. If you have any undigested food or gas in your gut, these stations compress it directly.

## Why Hyrox Specifically

A marathon is predictable. Your body learns a sustained aerobic effort pattern and your gut adapts.

Hyrox oscillates. Eight stations at maximal effort separated by running at aerobic pace. Your gut blood flow cycles between severely compromised (during stations) and partially restored (during running) — over and over. This cyclical disruption is harder to adapt to than sustained effort.

## The Fix Is Not What You Think

Most athletes try to fix this by eating less before racing. This helps slightly but misses the real problem.

The real problem is that your gut has never been trained to perform under these conditions.

Your gut is trainable. Exactly like a muscle. And most athletes have never trained it.

The solution is a 4-week gut adaptation protocol that progressively exposes your digestive system to increasing stress during training — teaching it to maintain function when blood flow is compromised.

I'll cover the full protocol in the next post. But the most important thing to understand today is this:

**GI distress during Hyrox is not bad luck. It is an untrained system.**

And untrained systems can be trained.
