**Book:** The Tipping Point — Malcolm Gladwell
**Read in:** 1 hour (library, speed-read, core ideas only)
**Verdict:** The framework holds. The examples are dated. The three principles are still the sharpest lens I've found for understanding why some things spread and others don't.

---

## The Three Rules

Gladwell's central claim: epidemics — whether disease, fashion, or ideas — tip from obscurity to ubiquity because of three things. Not a thousand things. Three.

### 1. The Law of the Few

Most of the work in spreading an idea is done by a tiny number of people. Not everyone spreads equally. Three types do the heavy lifting:

**Connectors** know an enormous number of people across different worlds. They are not influential because of expertise — they are influential because of reach. They are the bridges between communities that don't normally talk to each other.

**Mavens** are information specialists. They accumulate knowledge and — crucially — they want to share it. They are the ones who find the best restaurant, the best deal, the best product, and tell everyone they know. They spread because spreading feels good to them.

**Salesmen** are persuaders. They have a quality — hard to define, easy to feel — that makes you want to agree with them. They close the gap between "interesting" and "I'll try it."

The insight: you do not need everyone. You need the right few people. Find one Connector, one Maven, and one Salesman and your idea moves faster than any advertising campaign.

**What this means for me:** The Hyrox India community has exactly this structure. There are Connectors — athletes who know everyone across every box in three cities. There are Mavens — the obsessives who track every race result and will deep-dive into a personalized supplement stack, question every dosage, and then tell their entire training group about it. And there are Salesmen — the coaches whose athletes do whatever they recommend. I don't need to reach 5,000 Hyrox athletes. I need to find those three types of people and put something genuinely useful in their hands.

### 2. The Stickiness Factor

Spreading isn't enough. An idea has to stick — it has to be memorable and actionable enough that people don't immediately forget it after hearing it.

Gladwell's argument: stickiness is not about more information. It is about specific structural qualities of the message itself. Small changes to how something is presented can make the difference between forgotten and unforgettable.

His examples: Sesame Street discovered that children learn far better when humans and Muppets share the same screen rather than separate segments. Blue's Clues discovered that repetition — running the same episode five days in a row — dramatically increased comprehension. Neither of these is obvious. Both are counterintuitive.

Stickiness is testable. You can run it against real people and measure whether they remember it and act on it.

**What this means for me:** A generic supplement recommendation is not sticky. "Take creatine and magnesium" is forgotten by Tuesday. But "your stack — specifically yours, for your training load and your race schedule, with your timing guide" — that is sticky. The personalization is not just better science. It is better stickiness. The athlete remembers their stack because it is theirs.

The single stickiest format I have found: the timing guide card. AM / pre-workout / post-workout / PM — four moments, four supplements, one athlete's name at the top. That is the format to lead with.

### 3. The Power of Context

Gladwell's most underrated principle. Epidemics are sensitive to the conditions and circumstances of the time and place in which they occur. The same idea, in a different context, does not tip.

His most striking example: New York City's crime drop in the 1990s. He argues it was not primarily caused by demographic shifts or better policing strategy. It was caused by the broken windows policy — fixing subway graffiti, enforcing fare evasion — which changed the environmental cues that people were operating in. The context shifted. Behaviour followed.

People's behaviour is far more sensitive to their environment than we believe. We attribute behaviour to character when we should attribute it to context.

**What this means for me:** The Hyrox community is a context. Race day is a context. A WhatsApp group of athletes who just finished an event is a very different context from a cold Instagram ad. The same protocol, offered in the right context — in a Hyrox training group, right after someone posts about their race-day gut cramping — will tip. Offered in a generic health feed, it vanishes.

Timing and channel are not secondary considerations. They are the primary lever.

## The One Thing I Am Taking From This

Every tipping point needs a Connector to carry it to the right community, a sticky message that people remember and repeat, and the right contextual moment to offer it.

I have the product. The personalized supplement platform is real — AI-matched stacks, monthly delivery, continuously adjusted. The stickiness is the timing guide card: your name, your supplements, your four daily moments.

What I don't have yet: the Connectors. The two or three athletes in the Hyrox India community who are Mavens — who will try the personalized stack, feel the difference at Station 3, and tell everyone in their training group.

That is the next step. Not more features. Not a better website. Finding the three people who will carry it.
