**Book:** Losing My Virginity — Richard Branson
**Read in:** 1 hour (library, speed-read, key stories)
**Verdict:** The most honest founder autobiography I have read. Not a strategy book. A character book.

---

## The Core Idea

Branson has dyslexia. Left school at sixteen. Could not read financial statements for most of his career. Built Virgin into one of the most recognised brands on earth anyway.

The throughline across fifty years of building is one line he returns to repeatedly: **"Keep the fire burning. That is all that matters."**

Not the smartest product. Not the best-funded team. The fire. The founders who win are the ones who have not gone cold.

---

## Four Ideas That Stuck

### 1. Dyslexia as a Different Operating System

He could not read numbers, so he learned to read people. Every major decision Branson made was based on inputs a traditional operator would not even count — the energy in a room, whether he personally wanted to use the product, whether the person pitching him believed it.

In industries where culture beats analysis, this was an advantage. He entered airlines, mobile, and music — markets most analysts rated as traps — and won on brand and experience while competitors won on margin calculations.

### 2. *Student* Magazine — Selling the Future as the Present

His first business. He sold advertising by describing a readership he did not yet have. The magazine eventually died without value. He was locked in as chief editor by contract and had to watch it collapse from the inside.

What he got: a complete education in business failure before he was twenty. He came out knowing exactly what made it collapse — which was worth more than a clean exit would have been.

### 3. "Over My Dead Body" Is a Buy Signal

Every time Branson entered a new market, the incumbent told him he would fail. British Airways tried to destroy Virgin Atlantic through what became known as the "dirty tricks" campaign. The mobile carriers dismissed a music company's entry as absurd.

His read: the strength of the opposition is a signal about the size of the opportunity. Powerful incumbents do not fight businesses that do not threaten them. "You'll never pull this off" from someone who has something to lose is not a warning. It is confirmation.

### 4. Personal Risk as Brand-Building

He flew to Iraq to negotiate the release of hostages held by Saddam Hussein. He crossed the Atlantic in a hot air balloon — several failed attempts before breaking the record. He bet the airline against a loan that would have forced its sale if Virgin missed one payment.

Not stunts. A consistent pattern: a founder who personally absorbs risk sends a signal no marketing budget can replicate. Every balloon attempt, success or failure, generated weeks of global coverage and reinforced one message — Virgin is run by someone who actually believes what he says.

## The One Thing I Am Taking From This

Branson wrote hundreds of personal letters — to employees, to customers, to people who complained. Not form letters. Actual responses acknowledging the specific thing the person said.

We are about to get our first ten subscribers. Each of them made a ₹3,000 decision based on a promise. Branson's instinct: write each of them a personal message from the founder, tied to what they said in their intake form.

Not scalable. Exactly right at ten subscribers. Culture is set in the first ten decisions.
