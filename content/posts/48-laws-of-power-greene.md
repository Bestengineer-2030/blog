## How I Read This One

Different approach this time. Searched the summary on Google first, formed my own rough intuitions about what the laws might mean, then went and read. The learning came faster — I wasn't discovering ideas cold, I was testing what I already half-believed.

First read gives you maybe 30% of this book. Greene is writing about power at the level of patterns across centuries. You need repetition.

---

## The Six Laws That Stuck

### 1. No Days Unalert (Law 3: Conceal Your Intentions)

Greene's underlying point across many laws: most people are not paying attention. They are reacting to what people say rather than watching what they do.

Judge by actions. Not words, not explanations, not stated intentions — actions. The cost someone is willing to pay to get a result tells you everything about how much they actually want it.

This is hard to measure in the instant. Over weeks, it becomes obvious.

**What this means for me:** I have been taking people at their word too fast. An investor who says "send me a deck" is not an investor who wants to invest. An athlete who says "this protocol looks great" is not an athlete who will pay for coaching. Watch what people do next. That's the data.

---

### 2. When Asking for Help, Appeal to Self-Interest (Law 13)

Exact quote from Greene: "Appeal to people's self-interest, never to their mercy or gratitude."

When you ask for help by explaining your need — your struggle, your vision, your passion — you are asking someone to care about you. Most people don't. Not because they're cruel, but because they have their own problems.

When you ask for help by showing them what they get — what's in it for them, concretely — you are asking them to make a rational decision in their favour.

**What this means for me:** Every cold email to an investor or potential partner I've written has led with what I'm building. That's the wrong frame. The frame should be: what problem do they have that I can help with? A Hyrox coach has athletes who keep cramping at Station 3, who buy generic supplements that don't match their physiology. I have a platform that builds them a personalized stack. Lead with their problem. Earn the right to tell the rest of the story.

---

### 3. Crush the Enemy Totally (Law 15)

Greene's argument: leaving a defeated opponent half-destroyed is the most dangerous move. They recover. They remember. They come back.

He's not writing about physical enemies — he's writing about competitive dynamics. A competitor you leave breathing will rebuild. A bad habit you half-quit will return. A pattern of thinking you partly examine will reassert itself.

**What this means for me:** I have a habit of starting things and not finishing them. Half-quit. Half-crushed. The old thinking comes back because I never fully dismantled it. This law applies internally as much as externally.

---

### 4. Create an Air of Mystery (Law 3 / Law 6)

Greene: the more you explain yourself, the more ordinary you appear. Mystery creates power by making people fill in the gaps — usually in your favour.

Not manipulation. Editing. You don't have to share everything. Most people overshare anxiety and undershare results. Reverse it.

**What this means for me:** I talk too much about what I'm planning and too little about what I've already done. The platform architecture exists. The financial model exists. The team of five is assembled. Lead with what's real, not what's next. Let the output create its own mystery.

---

### 5. Concentrate Your Forces (Law 23)

This is the one I needed most.

Greene: "Conserve your forces and energies by keeping them concentrated at their strongest point." Dispersal is weakness. Intensity in one direction beats diffusion across ten.

I want to do too many things at once. The blog, the startup, the AI learning, the book challenge, the Hyrox training. All real. All important. All fighting each other for the same finite hours.

The law doesn't say stop. It says sequence. Pick the one thing that creates leverage for everything else. Concentrate. Then move.

**What this means for me:** The gut coaching startup is the one thing. Everything else — the blog, the AI learning — can serve it or wait. Right now I am spreading across all fronts simultaneously and advancing nowhere fast.

---

### 6. Assume Formlessness (Law 48)

The final law. Human creations evolve towards abstraction — more mental, less material. The most powerful position is to have no fixed form. You cannot be attacked where you cannot be found. You cannot be predicted when you have no pattern.

Greene references Mao: *"When we fight you, we make sure you don't get away."* That speed, concentration, and formlessness — striking from unexpected angles before the enemy can set their defence — is the military version of staying adaptable in business.

The lesson: when you commit to a rigid strategy, competitors and circumstances can counter it. Remain adaptable in tactics while clear in direction.

**What this means for me:** The personalized supplement platform has no fixed public form yet. We have not announced a pricing model, a launch date, or a market segment beyond Hyrox. That is not a weakness — it is an advantage. Test quietly with the first ten athletes. Move fast. Don't become a target before you're strong enough to hold the position.

---

## What I Disagree With

Greene writes as if power is the only variable worth optimising for. It isn't. Some of his laws — applied without restraint — produce people nobody trusts and nothing lasting.

The laws are descriptively accurate: this is how power actually moves in the world. Whether you should use all of them is a different question. The book is a lens, not a prescription. Use it to see clearly. Don't use it as an excuse to become someone you wouldn't want to meet.

---

## The One Thing I Am Taking From This

Concentrate your forces.

Not ten projects. Not even five. One thing, done with full intensity, until it tips. The gut coaching startup is that one thing. Everything else orbits it or waits.

Greene is telling me something I already knew and kept ignoring. Time to stop ignoring it.
